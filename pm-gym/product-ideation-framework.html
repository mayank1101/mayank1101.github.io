PM Gym

Product Ideation Framework

A repeatable path from insight to a shortlist worth building

Design-thinking ideation gives you the creative rules. A product ideation framework gives you the funnel: how a validated insight becomes a How Might We question, becomes a wide set of ideas, becomes a small set of concepts worth testing.

Step-by-step lessons

From Insight to Shortlist

Four short lessons: the ideation funnel, writing How Might We questions, generating ideas at product scale, and evaluating what you generate.

1

The Ideation Funnel

Product ideation works best as a funnel: it starts wide with evidence and narrows step by step toward something you can actually build and test.

1

Insight

A validated finding from research — the raw material everything else is built from.

2

How Might We question

The insight reframed as an open, optimistic prompt.

3

Ideas

A wide set generated from the HMW question — quantity over quality here.

4

Concepts

The strongest few ideas, fleshed out enough to evaluate properly.

5

Shortlist

Concepts ranked and ready for prototyping.

Quick check

Where does a validated user insight belong in the ideation funnel?

2

Writing How Might We Questions

A How Might We (HMW) question reframes an insight as an open, solution-neutral prompt. Scope it carefully: too broad and it produces noise; too narrow and it's already a solution in disguise.

Too broad

"How might we improve retention?"

Could point anywhere. Loses the specific insight it should be built from.

Well-scoped

"How might we help new sellers price with confidence?"

Grounded in a specific insight, open enough for many possible answers.

Too narrow

"How might we add a price-suggestion tooltip?"

Already names a solution — forecloses other ways to solve the same need.

Quick check

Which is the best-scoped HMW question, given the insight "new sellers abandon listing at the pricing step because they don't know competitive prices"?

3

Generating Ideas at Product Scale

Beyond open brainstorming, a few sources reliably produce stronger product ideas by pulling from outside the room or forcing a new angle.

Analogous inspiration

Borrow a proven pattern from a different industry solving a structurally similar problem.

Constraint-based ideation

"What if we had zero budget?" or "shipped in a day?" — artificial limits force unconventional ideas.

Mining customer language

Read support tickets and reviews for recurring suggestions — then reframe as an HMW, not a literal build order.

Quick check

A team studying restaurant reservation apps for inspiration on their own healthcare booking flow is using which technique?

4

Desirability, Feasibility, Viability

Once you have concepts, judge each on three lenses. A strong idea clears all three reasonably well — acing just one isn't enough.

Desirability

Do users actually want this? Grounded in research, not a hunch.

Feasibility

Can we build it with the technology, skills, and time we have?

Viability

Does it make sense for the business — cost, revenue, strategic fit?

Quick check

A brilliant idea scores extremely high on desirability but the team has no way to build it with current technology or skills. What lens is failing?
