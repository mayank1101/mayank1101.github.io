PM Gym

Ideation in Design Thinking

Generate wildly, then narrow deliberately

Ideation sits between defining the problem and building a prototype. Done well, it isn't a free-for-all — it's a disciplined swing between opening up as many possibilities as you can (diverge) and narrowing to the few worth testing (converge).

Step-by-step lessons

Diverge, Then Converge

Four short lessons: where ideation fits, the ground rules for brainstorming, a toolkit of techniques, and how to converge without killing good ideas.

1

Ideation's Place in Design Thinking

Design thinking runs through five stages: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test. Ideation is the hinge — it takes the sharp problem from Define and opens it wide into possible solutions, before Prototype narrows back down to something buildable.

1-2

Empathize & Define

Understand users, then frame a sharp problem statement.

3

Ideate

Diverge widely on solutions to that problem, then converge on a few worth trying.

4-5

Prototype & Test

Build a rough version of the strongest idea and check it with real users.

Skip straight from Define to Prototype and you anchor on the first idea instead of the best one.

Quick check

Why not skip ideation and prototype the first solution that comes to mind?

2

The Rules of Divergent Thinking

Good brainstorms follow ground rules that keep ideas flowing instead of shutting down early. Four matter most:

Rule 1

Defer judgment

No critiquing ideas during divergence — evaluation happens later, in a separate step.

Rule 2

Go for quantity

More ideas raise the odds a great one is in there. Quality gets sorted afterward.

Rule 3

Build on ideas

"Yes, and" — extend a teammate's idea rather than starting over or shutting it down.

Rule 4

Encourage wild ideas

An impossible idea can seed a feasible one. Welcome it before judging it.

Quick check

During a brainstorm, someone says "that would never work" about a wild idea. What's the ideation-rule violation?

3

A Toolkit of Ideation Techniques

Open brainstorming isn't the only way to diverge. A few structured techniques break specific bottlenecks — fixation, imbalance, or dominant voices.

Crazy 8s

Fold paper into 8 panels, sketch one idea per panel in 8 minutes — forces quantity and breaks fixation on the first idea.

SCAMPER

A checklist — Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to another use, Eliminate, Reverse — for pushing an existing idea in new directions.

Worst Possible Idea

Deliberately brainstorm terrible ideas to loosen inhibition, then flip them for a real insight.

Brainwriting

Write ideas silently first, then share — avoids groupthink and loud-voice bias.

Quick check

A team's brainstorms are always dominated by the two most senior, loudest voices. Which technique directly addresses this?

4

Converging Without Killing Good Ideas

Convergence is its own skill. Stop too early and you miss the idea buried mid-list; stop too late and you burn all your runway on options instead of building and testing one.

Dot voting, done right

Give each person a fixed number of votes to place on the sticky notes they find strongest — done silently, all at once, so early votes don't sway later ones. Then sort survivors by explicit criteria: feasibility, desirability, impact.

Convergence needs a method, same as divergence does. "Whatever feels right" just reintroduces the loudest-voice problem you just avoided.

Quick check

A facilitator ends ideation after five minutes because "we already have three good ideas." What's the risk?
