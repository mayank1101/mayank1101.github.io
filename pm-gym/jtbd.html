PM Gym

Jobs-to-be-Done

Why people really "hire" products — explained from zero

New to product management? Start here. Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) is the single most useful lens for understanding what users actually want. Learn it through stories, quick quizzes, flashcards, and a hands-on statement builder.

Step-by-step lessons

Understand the JTBD Mindset

No jargon, no prerequisites. Four short lessons with stories, examples, and a quick check after each one.

1

The Milkshake Story & the Core Idea

Here is the one idea everything else builds on: people don't buy products — they "hire" them to make progress in their life. Instead of asking "who is our customer?" (age, gender, income), ask "what situation are they stuck in, and what progress are they trying to make?"

Everyday example — the drill and the hole

There's a famous line: "People don't want a quarter-inch drill — they want a quarter-inch hole." And really, they don't even want the hole. They want to hang a photo of their kids so their living room feels like home. The drill is just something they "hired" to make that progress. If someone invented a cleaner way to hang the photo, the drill gets "fired." JTBD keeps your eyes on the progress people are chasing — because that's what they're loyal to, not your product.

The famous milkshake study

A fast-food chain wanted to sell more milkshakes. They asked customers what flavors they'd like. Nothing worked. Then researchers simply watched when people bought milkshakes — and discovered half were sold before 8:30 AM, to commuters.

The commuters had a problem: a long, boring drive. Bananas were gone in a minute, donuts made fingers sticky on the wheel, bagels were too dry. A thick milkshake lasted 25 minutes, fit the cupholder, and kept them full until lunch.

The "job" was: keep me occupied, clean, and full during my morning drive.

Imagine you work on Spotify. Which of these sounds like Jobs-to-be-Done thinking, rather than standard marketing?

2

The Anatomy of a Job Statement

Once you spot a job, you write it down in a specific format called a job statement. It turns a vague complaint ("this app is annoying") into something a team can actually build against. The format has three parts:

Everyday example — three parts, one sentence

Think of telling a friend why you grabbed takeout: "When I got home late and starving (situation), I wanted a hot meal with zero effort (motivation), so I could just eat and collapse on the sofa (outcome)." Same three parts every job statement uses: the situation you're in, the thing you want to do, and the deeper reason behind it. The trick is that the outcome ("collapse and relax") is the real target — the takeout is just one way to get there.

When…

The Situation

The real-life trigger. Where is the person? What's happening around them?

I want to…

The Motivation

The immediate thing they're trying to do to deal with that situation.

So I can…

The Outcome

The deeper reward — the convenience, relief, or feeling they're really after.

Quick check

You work on Netflix and you're writing a job statement for a tired parent: "When my kids are finally asleep on Friday night, I want to easily find a light, funny movie, so I can [_____]." Which option is the outcome?

3

The Three Dimensions of a Job

A job is never just about the practical task. Products people love work on three layers at once:

Everyday example — a luxury watch

Why pay thousands for a Rolex when a $20 watch tells time just as well? Because telling time (the functional job) is the least of it. Wearing it makes you feel successful and put-together (the emotional job), and it signals status to everyone who notices it (the social job). The cheap watch wins on function and loses everywhere else. Most products people love are quietly doing all three jobs at once — and beginners see only the first.

F

Functional — the practical task

The thing that literally needs doing: get from A to B, write a document, boil water.

E

Emotional — how they want to feel

The feeling they want (or want to avoid) while doing it: safe, calm, in control.

S

Social — how they want to be seen

What it says about them to others: professional, stylish, successful.

Quick check

Someone buys premium headphones, wears them in an open office, and listens to… nothing. They just want colleagues to think they're in deep-focus mode and leave them alone. Which dimension are they "hiring" the headphones for?

4

The Four Forces of Progress

Why don't people switch to your product even when it's clearly better? Because switching is a tug-of-war between four forces in their head. Two forces push them toward you, two hold them back:

Everyday example — switching gyms

Picture deciding whether to leave your gym for a new one. Push: your current gym is always crowded and the showers are broken. Pull: the new place has fancy equipment and a pool. Anxiety: what if the new one is a hassle to get to, or I'm locked into a contract? Habit: I know everyone at my old gym and my routine there. Even when the new gym is clearly better, you only switch when push + pull beat anxiety + habit. Great products don't just add pull — they actively shrink the anxiety and habit holding people back (free trials, easy imports, "cancel anytime").

Drives the switch

1. Push of the present

Frustration with what they use today. "My current tool keeps losing my edits!"

Drives the switch

2. Pull of the new

The appeal of your solution. "Wow, no downloads required!"

Holds them back

3. Anxiety of the new

Fear of switching. "What if this app loses my old files during setup?"

Holds them back

4. Habit of the present

Comfort with the current way. "But I already know all the shortcuts here…"

Quick check

A riddle: "I am the whisper of doubt right before someone clicks 'Sign Up.' I make them ask 'Will this take too long to learn?' and 'What if it breaks my current workflow?' I am the invisible anchor holding them back." Which force am I?

JTBD Quick Reference

The whole framework on one screen — perfect for a two-minute refresher.

The Four Forces

People switch when the promoters outweigh the blockers. Amplify the first two, shrink the last two.

Promoter

Push

Pain with the current way. "My workflow takes 4 hours."

Promoter

Pull

Appeal of the new solution. "This claims to do it in 1 minute."

Blocker

Anxiety

Fear of switching. "What if my old files get lost?"

Blocker

Habit

Comfort with today. "I already know these shortcuts."

Three Dimensions of a Job

Don't stop at functional. The emotional and social layers are where loyalty and word-of-mouth come from.

F

Functional

The practical task. "I want to boil water for cooking."

E

Emotional

How they feel. "I want to feel safe knowing the kettle switches itself off."

S

Social

How they're seen. "I want a designer kettle that impresses guests."

Rules of Thumb

1

Hire & fire

Adopting your product means firing something else — find out what.

2

Competitors are surprising

Netflix competes with wine and video games, not just other streaming apps.

3

Outcomes over outputs

Talk about the progress users make, not the features you ship.

Finding the Job (Interviewing)

Uncover jobs like a detective — chase the story, not a wish list.

Ask about the switch moment

"Walk me through the day you decided to buy / quit. What happened right before?"

Avoid "what features do you want?"

Users guess at solutions badly. Ask about their situation and struggle instead.

Find what they "fired"

What were they using before, and why did it stop working?

Dig past functional

Ask "and why does that matter?" until you hit the emotional/social job.
