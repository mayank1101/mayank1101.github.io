PM Gym

Mind Maps

Think in branches, not bullet points

Some problems are too tangled for a linear list. A mind map starts from one central idea and lets you branch outward freely, mirroring how ideas actually associate in your head — useful for brainstorms, meeting notes, and untangling a fuzzy problem space.

Step-by-step lessons

Branch Out From the Center

Four short lessons: what a mind map is, how to build one, where PMs actually use them, and when a different tool fits better.

1

A Radial Way to Think

A mind map starts with one central idea and radiates outward into branches and sub-branches. Unlike a strict outline, it mirrors how thoughts actually connect — related ideas can sit near each other without being forced into one linear order.

Everyday example — a subway map, not a train schedule

A train schedule is linear: stop 1, then 2, then 3. A subway map is radial: lines branch out from hubs, and you can see how far-apart stations relate without following one strict sequence. A mind map does for ideas what a subway map does for stations.

2

How to Build a Mind Map

A mind map's speed comes from its discipline: a clear center, a few main branches, and short labels you can scan at a glance.

1

Start with the central node

The topic or problem, in a few words or a simple image.

2

Draw main branches

The major sub-themes, radiating close to the center.

3

Add sub-branches

Details off each main branch — keep them to keywords, not sentences.

4

Group with color or icons

Visually cluster related branches so patterns jump out.

Keep each branch to a keyword or short phrase — full sentences kill the associative speed that makes mind maps useful.

Quick check

A teammate fills every branch of a mind map with full paragraphs of text. What's the issue?

3

Where PMs Actually Use Mind Maps

Mind maps earn their keep in a few specific moments — mostly when things are still messy and you're not ready for a rigid structure yet.

Brainstorming

Radiate possible solutions out from a central How Might We question.

Structuring a fuzzy problem

Explore an ill-defined area before it's sharp enough for a formal problem statement.

Meeting or research notes

Capture a messy conversation without forcing premature structure.

Feature or scope breakdown

Branch a big feature into its component parts.

Quick check

A team has a fuzzy, not-yet-defined problem area ("our onboarding feels off somehow") and needs to explore it before writing a sharp problem statement. What tool fits this early stage well?

4

Mind Maps vs. Other Tools

A mind map isn't the right shape for every job. Knowing when to reach for something else matters as much as knowing how to build one.

Open exploration

Mind Map

Best for radiating freely from one fuzzy topic before anything is defined.

Defined metric

KPI Tree

Best for mathematically decomposing one metric into the drivers that produce it.

Many raw data points

Affinity Map

Best for clustering many individual quotes or notes bottom-up into emergent themes.

Quick check

A researcher has 200 sticky notes of raw user quotes from interviews and needs to find recurring themes. Which tool fits better, a mind map or an affinity map?
