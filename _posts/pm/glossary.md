Your first week learning product management can feel like someone is speaking a different language.

"We need to prioritise the backlog based on KPIs before the sprint review, and the PM wants an OKR aligned to our North Star metric by EOD."

What?

Every field has its own vocabulary. Product management has more than most, because it sits at the intersection of business, design, and technology — borrowing jargon from all three. The good news is that once you know what these words actually mean, none of them are complicated.

This glossary covers every term you're likely to encounter as a beginner. Each one is explained in plain language, with a real example. No fluff. No assumed knowledge.

Bookmark this page. Come back to it whenever something in a lecture, article, or meeting doesn't make sense.

## The Basics {#the-basics}

### Product Manager (PM)

The person responsible for deciding *what* a product should do and *why* — but not necessarily *how* to build it. A PM works with designers, engineers, and business teams to make sure the product solves real problems for real people.

Think of a PM like a film director. They don't write the code (act), design the screens (do camera work), or write the marketing copy (handle post-production). But they're responsible for making sure all those people are building the *right thing* together.

---

### Product

In product management, a "product" usually means a digital product — an app, a website, or a software service. But the same ideas apply to physical products too.

*Example: Spotify is a product. WhatsApp is a product. The Gmail app on your phone is a product.*

---

### Feature

A specific piece of functionality in a product. A single product has many features.

*Example: Instagram's "Stories" is a feature. The ability to send a voice message on WhatsApp is a feature. The dark mode on an app is a feature.*

---

### User vs. Customer

These two words get mixed up constantly, so it's worth being clear.

A **user** is someone who uses the product. A **customer** is someone who pays for it. They're often the same person, but not always.

*Example: If a company pays for Slack so their employees can communicate, the company is the customer (they pay the bill) and the employees are the users (they use Slack every day). The employees didn't choose to buy Slack, their employer did.*

This matters because users and customers can want very different things.

---

### Stakeholder

Anyone who has an interest in, or is affected by, what the product team does. This includes people inside the company (your manager, the sales team, the engineers) and outside it (users, investors, partner companies).

*Example: If you're building a new checkout feature for an e-commerce app, your stakeholders include the engineering team who builds it, the finance team who processes payments, the marketing team who'll announce it, and the customers who'll actually use it.*

Managing stakeholders — keeping them informed, understanding what they need, resolving conflicting opinions, is one of the core practical skills of a PM.

---

### Problem Statement

A clear description of the specific problem you are trying to solve, written *before* you decide on a solution. Good PMs start here. Bad ones jump straight to solutions.

*Example: A bad problem statement: "We need to add a chat feature." That's a solution, not a problem.*

*A good problem statement: "Users who need help during checkout have no way to get it immediately, so they abandon their purchase. We need to find a way to give users real-time help at the moment they're confused."*

The difference matters because the chat feature is just one possible solution. A better solution might be simplifying the checkout so users don't get confused in the first place.

---

### Go-to-Market (GTM)

The plan for how you'll release something to the world. It covers who the product is for, how people will find out about it, how it will be sold, and how it will be priced.

*Example: When Apple releases a new iPhone, they don't just put it on the website and hope for the best. They do a launch event, send devices to reviewers, run TV ads, open pre-orders, and coordinate with mobile carriers. That whole plan is the go-to-market strategy.*

## Users and Customers {#users-and-customers}

### User Persona

A fictional character that represents a typical type of user. Personas are created by doing research with real users and then summarising what you learned into a character that the whole team can refer to when making decisions.

*Example: "Priya, 28, works as a freelance graphic designer. She uses five different apps to track client projects, invoice clients, and manage her schedule. She's frustrated that none of them talk to each other and she wastes 30 minutes a day on manual data entry."*

Priya isn't a real person, but she represents a real type of user. When the team debates a design decision, someone can ask "what would Priya think of this?" and get a useful answer.

---

### User Journey (or Customer Journey)

The series of steps a user goes through to accomplish something with your product — from first hearing about it, to signing up, to completing a task, to coming back again.

*Example: Here's a simplified user journey for someone finding and booking a restaurant on an app:*

*Feels hungry → opens the app → searches by location → browses options → reads reviews → taps "Book a table" → enters details → receives confirmation → goes to the restaurant → (maybe) leaves a review.*

Each step in this journey is an opportunity for something to go right or wrong. Mapping it out helps you find where users get stuck or frustrated.

---

### Pain Point

A problem, frustration, or difficulty that a user experiences — either with your product specifically, or with the broader situation your product is trying to help with.

*Example: If you have to re-enter your card details every time you buy something on an app, that's a pain point. If searching for flights always requires visiting four different websites to compare prices, that's a pain point. If a cooking app gives you recipes but never tells you which ingredients you already have, that's a pain point.*

Finding real pain points — through research, interviews, and observation — is one of the most important things a PM does.

---

### User Research

The practice of learning directly from users — watching them use your product, interviewing them, asking them questions — to understand what they actually need, rather than guessing.

*Example: You think users are leaving your app because the design is ugly. You do five user interviews and discover they're actually leaving because they don't understand what the product does in the first 60 seconds. Without research, you'd have spent three months redesigning the wrong thing.*

User research can be as simple as a 20-minute conversation with someone who uses your product, or as structured as a formal survey with hundreds of responses.

---

### Onboarding

The experience a new user has when they first start using your product — the steps that help them get set up, understand what the product does, and experience its value for the first time.

*Example: When you first open Duolingo, it asks you which language you want to learn, why you want to learn it, and how much time you can commit each day. Then it walks you through a first mini-lesson. By the end of your first five minutes, you've already done something useful. That entire experience is the onboarding.*

Onboarding is critical because users who don't understand a product quickly will simply stop using it.

---

### Churn

When a user or customer stops using your product — they cancel their subscription, delete the app, or just disappear.

*Example: If a music streaming service starts the month with 1,000 subscribers and ends with 950, 50 people churned.*

High churn is one of the biggest problems a product can have, because you're constantly losing people you've already won. Reducing churn — understanding *why* people leave and fixing those reasons — is often more valuable than spending money to attract new users.

---

### Retention

The opposite of churn. How well you keep users coming back over time.

*Example: If 100 people sign up for your app in January, and 40 of them are still actively using it in March, your 2-month retention rate is 40%.*

Retention is one of the clearest signals that a product is actually delivering value. Users don't come back to things that aren't useful to them.

---

### Aha Moment

The specific moment in a user's experience when they first truly understand and feel the value of your product. Finding and accelerating this moment is one of the most powerful things a PM can do.

*Example: For Facebook, researchers found the "aha moment" was when a new user added 7 friends within 10 days. Users who did this were dramatically more likely to stay active. Users who didn't, drifted away.*

*For Dropbox, the aha moment was the first time a user saved a file and then accessed it from a different device. That was the moment the value clicked.*

---

### Feedback Loop

When the result of something feeds back into the system and influences what happens next. Products with good feedback loops get better the more people use them.

*Example: More reviews on a restaurant app → the app gets more useful for everyone → more people use the app → more reviews. That's a positive feedback loop.*

We go deep on feedback loops in the next article in this series.

## Planning and Prioritisation {#planning-and-prioritisation}

### Product Roadmap

A plan that shows what the product team is going to build, and roughly when. A roadmap communicates direction and priorities — it's not a rigid schedule.

*Example: A roadmap might say "Q1: Improve onboarding. Q2: Launch mobile app. Q3: Add payment integrations." It tells everyone what's coming, without committing to exact dates for each feature.*

A common mistake is treating a roadmap like a promise. Good PMs treat it like a current best guess — one that changes as they learn more.

---

### Backlog

The full list of all the features, improvements, bug fixes, and tasks that the team has identified but hasn't built yet. The backlog is usually very long. Prioritising it — deciding what goes first — is one of the most important and difficult PM responsibilities.

*Example: Imagine a backlog with 200 items on it: add dark mode, fix the login bug, improve the search results, add a feedback form, support more languages, improve loading speed... Every team has a backlog like this. The PM's job is to figure out which 10 items will make the biggest difference right now.*

---

### Prioritisation

The process of deciding which problems to solve and which features to build — and in what order. Because teams always have more ideas than time, prioritisation is a constant, unavoidable part of product management.

Several frameworks help with this:

**Impact vs. Effort** — Rate each item by how much value it delivers and how hard it is to build. High impact, low effort items go first.

**RICE** — Stands for Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort. A scoring system to make prioritisation more objective.

**MoSCoW** — Sorts features into Must have, Should have, Could have, and Won't have.

None of these frameworks removes the need for judgment. They just make your reasoning more visible.

---

### MVP (Minimum Viable Product)

The simplest, smallest version of a product that still delivers enough value to be useful — and that you can actually build and release quickly in order to learn from real users.

*Example: Before building a full restaurant booking app, you could test the idea by creating a simple website where people fill in a form and you manually call the restaurant to make the booking. It's not automated, it doesn't scale, but it lets you find out if people actually want this thing before you spend months building software.*

The point of an MVP is to learn as fast as possible with as little waste as possible.

---

### Sprint

A fixed period of time (usually one or two weeks) during which a team focuses on building a defined set of things. At the end of the sprint, the team reviews what they built and plans the next sprint.

*Example: "This sprint, we're building the password reset flow and fixing the three most common bugs." Two weeks later, those things are done (or not), and the team decides what to focus on next.*

Working in sprints keeps teams focused, creates regular checkpoints, and makes it easier to change direction when needed.

---

### User Story

A short description of a feature or requirement written from the perspective of the person using it. The standard format is: "As a [type of user], I want [to do something], so that [I get some benefit]."

*Example: "As a first-time visitor, I want to see example recipes without signing up, so that I can decide if this app is worth joining."*

User stories keep the focus on the user's goal rather than on technical specifications. They're a way of asking "why does this person need this?" before deciding how to build it.

---

### Acceptance Criteria

The specific conditions that must be met for a feature to be considered done. Written alongside user stories to make sure everyone agrees on what "finished" actually means.

*Example: User story: "As a user, I want to reset my password so that I can log in if I forget it."*

*Acceptance criteria:*

- *User receives an email within 2 minutes of requesting a reset*
- *The reset link expires after 24 hours*
- *After resetting, the user is taken directly to the home screen*
- *The old password no longer works after reset*

Without acceptance criteria, "done" means different things to different people.

---

### A/B Test

An experiment where you show two different versions of something to different groups of users — version A to one group, version B to another — and measure which one performs better.

*Example: You want to know if a green "Sign Up" button gets more clicks than a blue one. You show the green button to 50% of visitors and the blue one to the other 50%, for two weeks. At the end, the green button had 18% more clicks. You ship the green button.*

A/B testing removes guesswork from design decisions. Instead of arguing about which version is better, you let users tell you.

## Metrics — How You Measure Success {#metrics}

### Metric / KPI

A **metric** is any number you track. A **KPI** (Key Performance Indicator) is a metric that specifically measures whether you're achieving your goals. KPIs are the metrics that matter most.

*Example: An e-commerce app might track hundreds of metrics (page load time, number of searches, items viewed...) but their key KPIs might be: number of completed purchases per day, average order value, and the percentage of visitors who make a purchase.*

Not all metrics are KPIs. A good PM knows the difference between a number that's interesting and a number that actually tells them whether the product is healthy.

---

### DAU / MAU (Daily and Monthly Active Users)

- **DAU** = the number of unique people who use your product on a given day
- **MAU** = the number of unique people who use your product in a given month

*Example: If 10,000 different people open your app on Tuesday, your DAU is 10,000. If 80,000 different people use it at least once in June, your MAU is 80,000.*

The ratio of DAU to MAU (DAU/MAU) tells you how frequently people return. An app with 10,000 DAU and 100,000 MAU has a 10% ratio — people come back about 3 times a month. An app with 70,000 DAU and 100,000 MAU has a 70% ratio — people come back nearly every day. The second app is much stickier.

---

### Conversion Rate

The percentage of people who complete a desired action — out of all the people who had the opportunity to complete it.

*Example: If 1,000 people visit your app's sign-up page and 150 of them actually sign up, your conversion rate is 15%.*

*If 500 people add an item to their cart and 200 of them complete the purchase, your checkout conversion rate is 40%.*

Conversion rates are everywhere in product work: sign-up conversion, trial-to-paid conversion, click-through rate, and so on.

---

### Retention Rate

The percentage of users who continue using your product after a certain amount of time.

*Example: If 100 people sign up in January, and 30 of them are still active in February, your 30-day retention rate is 30%.*

Retention is often measured at Day 1, Day 7, Day 30, and Day 90. Each drop-off point tells you something different about where users are losing interest.

---

### Churn Rate

The percentage of users or customers who stop using your product in a given period.

*Example: If you start the month with 1,000 subscribers and 50 cancel, your monthly churn rate is 5%.*

Churn and retention are two ways of measuring the same thing. If your monthly retention is 95%, your monthly churn is 5%.

---

### NPS (Net Promoter Score)

A measure of how likely your users are to recommend your product to someone they know. Users are asked: "On a scale of 0–10, how likely are you to recommend this to a friend?" Based on their answers:

- **9–10**: Promoters (they love it and will spread the word)
- **7–8**: Passives (they're okay with it but won't really advocate for it)
- **0–6**: Detractors (they're unhappy and might warn others away)

NPS = % of Promoters − % of Detractors. Scores range from −100 to +100. Anything above 50 is considered excellent.

*Example: If 60% of your users are Promoters and 10% are Detractors, your NPS is 50.*

NPS is a quick way to take the temperature of how users feel overall — not a deep diagnosis of specific problems.

---

### North Star Metric

The single most important number that the whole company or team rallies around. It represents the core value the product delivers to users, not just a business outcome.

*Example: For Airbnb, the North Star Metric was "nights booked." Not revenue, not sign-ups — but the actual thing at the centre of the product's value: people staying in homes.*

*For Spotify, it's reportedly "time spent listening."*

*For a health app, it might be "days users logged their food."*

Having a North Star Metric gives everyone a shared answer to "are we making the product better for users?" It prevents teams from chasing metrics that look good on paper but don't actually reflect real value.

---

### Funnel

A visual model of the steps users take to reach a goal — wider at the top (many users entering) and narrowing at the bottom (fewer completing). People drop off at each step.

*Example: A typical sign-up funnel might look like this:*

```text
10,000  people visit the landing page
  │
  ▼
 4,000  click "Sign Up"
  │
  ▼
 2,500  fill in their email
  │
  ▼
 1,800  confirm their email
  │
  ▼
   900  complete their profile and become active users
```

At each step, people drop off. A PM's job is to understand why people leave at each stage and fix the biggest drops first.

---

### Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

A **lagging indicator** measures the result of something — it tells you what already happened.
A **leading indicator** measures something that predicts future results — it tells you what's likely to happen.

*Example: Revenue is a lagging indicator. It tells you how the product did last month.*

*The number of new free-trial sign-ups this week is a leading indicator. It tells you roughly how many paying customers you're likely to have in 30 days.*

Leading indicators are valuable because they give you time to act. By the time a lagging indicator looks bad, it may be too late to change it.

## The Building Process {#the-building-process}

### Agile

A way of organising how a team works — by building in small pieces, getting feedback often, and continuously improving. The opposite of building everything in one large batch and releasing it a year later.

*Example: Instead of spending 18 months designing and building a perfect app and then releasing it, an agile team might release a basic version in 6 weeks, get feedback from real users, and then improve it over many subsequent releases.*

Agile teams move faster, fail smaller, and learn quicker.

---

### Scrum

One popular way of doing agile. Scrum organises work into sprints (usually two weeks), with regular meetings to plan, review, and improve.

Common Scrum rituals:

- **Sprint Planning**: deciding what to build in the next sprint
- **Daily Stand-up**: a short daily meeting where each person says what they did, what they're doing, and if anything is blocking them
- **Sprint Review**: showing what was built at the end of a sprint
- **Retrospective**: the team reflects on how they worked and what to improve

---

### Bug

Something in the product that doesn't work the way it's supposed to.

*Example: A button that's supposed to submit a form but does nothing when clicked is a bug. An app that crashes when you rotate your phone is a bug. A feature that shows you someone else's order history is a very serious bug.*

Bugs are a normal part of software development. Managing which bugs to fix first — based on how many users are affected and how badly — is part of the PM's job.

---

### QA (Quality Assurance)

The process of testing a product to find bugs and problems before it reaches users. QA can be done by a dedicated testing team, by engineers, by the PM, or by all three.

*Example: Before releasing a new checkout flow, the QA team tests it on 20 different devices, in 5 different browsers, with 10 different payment methods, with a slow internet connection, and with an expired credit card — to find every way it could break before real users do.*

---

### Release / Launch / Deployment

These three words are often used interchangeably but have slightly different meanings:

- **Deployment**: the technical act of putting new code onto the server so it's live
- **Release**: making a new version of the product available to users
- **Launch**: releasing something significant, usually with marketing and announcements attached

*Example: An engineering team might deploy code to servers every day. They release new features to users once a week. They launch — with press coverage and a campaign — when something major ships.*

---

### Feature Flag

A way to turn a feature on or off for specific users without deploying new code. This lets teams release features to a small group first (say, 5% of users) to test them before rolling out to everyone.

*Example: A team builds a new search experience. Instead of releasing it to all 2 million users at once, they switch it on for 10,000 users first. They watch the data for a week. If it works well, they increase to 100,000, then 1 million, then everyone.*

Feature flags make it much safer to experiment and much easier to reverse a bad decision.

## Strategy Terms {#strategy-terms}

### Product Vision

A description of what the product is trying to become in the long run — the big, inspiring goal that guides all decisions.

*Example: Amazon's early vision was "every book ever published, available in 60 seconds." Google's was "to organise the world's information and make it universally accessible."*

A good product vision is inspiring enough to motivate people, specific enough to guide decisions, and broad enough to allow many different approaches.

---

### Product Strategy

The approach you'll take to achieve the vision. Strategy answers: who is this for, what problem does it solve, and how are we different from alternatives?

*Example: Vision: "Help people feel in control of their personal finances." Strategy: "Focus on people aged 22–35 who are earning their first real salary and feel overwhelmed by budgeting apps that are too complex. Build something simpler and more encouraging than anything currently available."*

Strategy is not a list of features. It's a choice about focus.

---

### OKR (Objectives and Key Results)

A goal-setting framework. An **Objective** is the qualitative goal — what you want to achieve. **Key Results** are the specific, measurable outcomes that tell you whether you're getting there.

*Example:*

*Objective: Make onboarding feel welcoming and quick for new users.*

*Key Results:*

- *80% of new users complete their profile within 24 hours of signing up (up from 45%)*
- *The average time to complete onboarding drops from 8 minutes to 4 minutes*
- *7-day retention for new users increases from 30% to 45%*

OKRs are powerful because they separate the goal (what you want) from the measurement (how you'll know you got there). Teams that only set goals without defining how they'll measure success often drift.

---

### Product-Market Fit

The point at which a product truly solves a real problem for a real group of people — well enough that those people would genuinely miss it if it disappeared.

*Example: A simple way to test for product-market fit: ask users "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" If more than 40% say "very disappointed," you likely have product-market fit. If most people say "a bit disappointed" or "not bothered," you probably don't.*

Before product-market fit, a company's job is to find it. After product-market fit, the job becomes growing and scaling it. Trying to scale before you have product-market fit is one of the most common and expensive startup mistakes.

---

### Pivot

When a company significantly changes its product, target audience, or business model based on what it has learned — rather than giving up or persisting with something that isn't working.

*Example: YouTube started as a video dating site. When they noticed people were uploading all kinds of videos — not just dating profiles — they pivoted to become a general video sharing platform.*

*Instagram started as a location check-in app called Burbn. When they noticed users mostly just used the photo-sharing part, they stripped everything else out and pivoted to focus on photos.*

A pivot is not failure — it's a deliberate change of direction based on evidence.

---

### Product-Led Growth (PLG)

A go-to-market strategy where the product itself is the main driver of user acquisition, conversion, and expansion — rather than sales teams or advertising.

*Example: Slack, Figma, Notion, and Dropbox are all product-led growth companies. You don't need to talk to a salesperson to start using them. You sign up for free, experience value quickly, and the product itself naturally leads you toward paying for more.*

The idea is: if the product is good enough, users become the sales force.

---

### North Star Framework

Expanding on the North Star Metric — this framework says every feature, project, and initiative should connect clearly to moving the North Star. If a proposed feature can't be connected to the North Star Metric, it's worth questioning whether it should be built at all.

*Example: If Spotify's North Star is "time spent listening," then improving the recommendation algorithm (helps users find music they like → more listening) is clearly connected. Adding a feature to share your listening stats publicly is less obviously connected. The framework forces the team to ask: does this actually make users listen more?*

## Business and Revenue {#business-and-revenue}

### B2C (Business to Consumer)

A product sold directly to individual people for their personal use.

*Example: Netflix, Instagram, Spotify, and Uber are all B2C products. You are the customer and the user.*

---

### B2B (Business to Business)

A product sold to organisations and companies, usually used by employees as part of their work.

*Example: Slack, Salesforce, and Jira are B2B products. A company buys them for their team to use. The company is the customer; the employees are the users.*

B2B products often have longer, more complex sales processes and higher prices, but also longer-term customer relationships.

---

### SaaS (Software as a Service)

A software product that you access over the internet and typically pay for on a subscription basis — monthly or yearly — rather than buying once and installing on your computer.

*Example: Google Docs, Dropbox, Zoom, and Notion are all SaaS products. You don't buy them once; you pay a subscription to keep using them. The company is responsible for maintaining, updating, and hosting the software.*

Most modern software products are SaaS.

---

### Freemium

A pricing model where the basic version of the product is free, and users pay for access to premium features.

*Example: Spotify is free with ads and shuffle-only playback. Pay for Premium and you get no ads, on-demand listening, and offline downloads. The free tier attracts a huge audience; the premium tier generates revenue from the people who want more.*

The challenge with freemium is converting enough free users into paying ones. If the free version is too good, nobody upgrades. If it's too limited, nobody signs up in the first place.

---

### Revenue

The money a product brings in. Simple as that.

*Example: If 1,000 users each pay £10/month for your product, your monthly revenue is £10,000.*

Revenue is not the same as profit. Revenue is the money coming in; profit is what's left after you subtract costs.

---

### LTV (Lifetime Value)

The total amount of money a customer is expected to spend on your product over the entire time they remain a customer.

*Example: If a user pays £10/month and stays for an average of 24 months before cancelling, their LTV is £240.*

LTV matters because it tells you how much you can afford to spend to acquire a customer.

---

### CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)

The average amount you spend to win one new customer — including advertising, sales team time, and any other costs related to bringing new customers in.

*Example: If you spend £5,000 on ads in a month and get 100 new customers, your CAC is £50.*

The relationship between LTV and CAC is one of the most fundamental ratios in any product business. If your LTV is £240 and your CAC is £50, you make £190 per customer over their lifetime — healthy. If your CAC is £300, you're losing money on every customer you acquire — not healthy.

---

### Monetisation

How a product makes money. Different products monetise in very different ways.

Common monetisation models:

- **Subscription** — users pay a regular fee (Spotify, Netflix)
- **Freemium** — basic is free, premium is paid (Dropbox, LinkedIn)
- **Advertising** — the product is free; advertisers pay to reach users (Facebook, YouTube)
- **Marketplace commission** — the platform takes a cut of each transaction (Airbnb, Uber, Amazon Marketplace)
- **One-time purchase** — pay once, own forever (most older software, many mobile games)

*Example: Instagram is free for users because it monetises through advertising. Advertisers pay Instagram to show ads to users. Users are the product, not the customer.*

---

### Unit Economics

A fancy way of saying: "does the business make money on each individual customer?" Specifically, does LTV > CAC?

*Example: If acquiring a customer costs £20 and that customer generates £80 over their lifetime, the unit economics are positive (you make £60 per customer). If acquiring a customer costs £100 and they only generate £60, the unit economics are negative — you lose money on every customer, and growing faster just means losing money faster.*

Good unit economics are essential before scaling. Scaling a business with bad unit economics is like trying to fill a leaky bathtub by running the tap faster.

| Freemium | Free basic version, paid premium version |
| Roadmap | A plan showing what you'll build and roughly when |
