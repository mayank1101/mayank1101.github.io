Imagine you receive ten thousand customer support emails in a single week.

Inside those emails, customers are mentioning products by name, talking about cities where they had bad delivery experiences, naming specific staff members who helped them, citing dates when things went wrong, and referencing invoice numbers.

Now imagine someone on your team has to read all ten thousand emails and manually pull out every product name, city, person, and date mentioned. They'd need weeks. And they'd still make mistakes.

Named Entity Recognition — NER, for short, is the technology that does this automatically, in seconds, with high accuracy.

It's one of the most practically useful AI tools a product manager can understand. Not because you'll build it yourself, but because once you know what it does, you start seeing opportunities for it everywhere in your product.

---

## What Exactly Is a "Named Entity"?

Before we get to "recognition," let's understand what we're recognising.

A **named entity** is a real-world thing that has a name. Things like:

- **People** — Elon Musk, Sundar Pichai, your customer service rep Maria
- **Places** — London, Mumbai, Heathrow Airport, Pacific Ocean
- **Organisations** — Apple, the UN, Goldman Sachs, NHS
- **Dates and Times** — 14 June 2026, next Tuesday, Q3 2025, 3:45 PM
- **Money** — $500, ₹2,000, €89.99
- **Products** — iPhone 15, Galaxy S24, Adobe Photoshop
- **Events** — the FIFA World Cup, Black Friday, the 2024 Olympics

When a piece of text mentions any of these things, that mention is a **named entity**.

---

## So What Is Named Entity Recognition?

Named Entity Recognition is the process of reading a piece of text and automatically identifying and recognising all the named entities in it, and labelling each one by type.

Here's a simple example.

**Input text:**
> "Sundar Pichai announced at Google I/O that the new Pixel phone will launch in October 2026 for $799."

**After NER:**
- *Sundar Pichai* → **PERSON**
- *Google I/O* → **EVENT**
- *Pixel* → **PRODUCT**
- *October 2026* → **DATE**
- *$799* → **MONEY**

That's it. The technology reads the sentence and tags each important thing with a label.

The power comes from doing this across thousands or millions of pieces of text, instantly.

---

## Why Should a Product Manager Care?

Here's the honest answer: most of the valuable data your users generate is **unstructured text**.

Customer reviews. Support tickets. Survey responses. App store feedback. Social media comments. Chat messages. Emails.

None of that data comes in a nice table with columns. It's all just words. And words, on their own, are very hard for a computer to analyse at scale.

NER turns unstructured text into structured, searchable, actionable data.

That shifts the focus from messy words to organised information is where the product opportunity lives.

## Real-World Examples That PMs Will Recognise

### 1. Customer Support Automation

A user writes: *"My order #A4421 placed on 10 June hasn't arrived and I spoke to your agent James twice already."*

Without NER, this is just a long string of text. A support system has to route it manually or use keyword matching.

With NER, the system automatically extracts:
- **Order ID**: A4421
- **Date**: 10 June
- **Person**: James

Now the ticket can be instantly routed to the right team, pre-filled with the relevant context, and linked to the order history without anyone reading it first.

### 2. News and Content Monitoring

A media monitoring product needs to track every time a specific company gets mentioned in the news.

NER lets the product tag every article with the organisations, people, and locations it discusses. Users can then filter, search, and set alerts like *"notify me whenever Tesla or Elon Musk is mentioned in a negative article about product safety."*

### 3. Healthcare and Medical Records

Doctors write notes in natural language. Those notes mention drug names, dosages, diagnoses, patient ages, and dates.

NER can extract all of those entities automatically, turning handwritten clinical notes into structured data that can power better patient records, drug interaction warnings, and research insights.

### 4. Financial Services

Earnings call transcripts mention competitor names, geographic markets, financial figures, and product lines. NER lets analysts automatically pull all of this out of hours of spoken-word content without listening to every call themselves.

### 5. Hiring and Recruitment

A recruiter pastes in a CV. NER identifies universities, companies the candidate has worked at, job titles, years of experience, and technical skills mentioned. This can auto-populate a candidate profile without manual data entry.

## How Does It Actually Work? (The Simple Version)

You don't need to understand the technical details to use NER well as a PM. But a rough mental model helps when talking to engineers or evaluating vendors.

### Step 1: The model learns from examples

Imagine you showed someone thousands of sentences, and for each sentence, you had already highlighted and labelled every named entity.

*"Barack Obama was the 44th president of the United States."* → Barack Obama = PERSON, United States = LOCATION

After seeing enough examples, the person would start to get good at spotting names, places, and organisations in new sentences they'd never seen before.

That's essentially what NER models do. They learn from thousands of labelled examples and become very good at identifying entities in new, unseen text.

### Step 2: Context matters, not just the words

Here's something interesting: the word "Apple" could be a fruit, a technology company, or part of a person's name (like Apple Martin, Gwyneth Paltrow's daughter).

A good NER model reads the surrounding context to figure out which one it is.

*"Apple reported record profits this quarter"* → Apple = ORGANISATION
*"She packed an apple in her lunchbox"* → apple = not a named entity
*"Apple Martin attended the premiere"* → Apple = PERSON

This is why NER is more sophisticated than simple word matching.

### Step 3: It gives you labels, not answers

NER doesn't tell you *what to do* with the entities it finds. It just identifies and labels them. The product logic that decides what to do when a customer mentions a specific person, or when a review mentions a competitor is still designed by you.

## What Does "Accuracy" Mean in NER?

When engineers talk about how well an NER model performs, they use a few terms worth knowing.

**Precision** — When the model says something is a named entity, how often is it right?

Think of it like this: if the model tagged 100 things as company names, and 90 of them were actually company names, precision is 90%.

**Recall** — Out of all the named entities that actually exist in the text, how many did the model successfully find?

If there were 120 company names in the text and the model found 90 of them, recall is 75%.

**F1 Score** — A single number that balances precision and recall. Higher is better. Most state-of-the-art NER models score above 90% on standard benchmarks.

As a PM, you don't need to calculate these yourself. But you should ask your team what precision and recall look like on *your specific data*, not on generic benchmarks. A model that's 93% accurate on news articles might be 70% accurate on your customers' informal chat messages.

## The Things That Can Go Wrong (And What To Do About Them)

### Problem 1: The model was trained on different kinds of text than yours

A model trained on formal news articles might struggle with casual chat messages full of abbreviations, slang, and typos. *"got my iphone 15 but smtg wrong with the cam, talked to tom at store"* is harder than a press release.

**What to do:** Test the model on real samples of your actual data before committing. If accuracy is low, consider fine-tuning the model with examples from your domain.

### Problem 2: Custom entities your model doesn't know about

Generic NER models know about common entity types — people, places, organisations. But your product might care about very specific things: internal product codes, proprietary brand names, medical terminology unique to your company, or industry jargon.

**What to do:** Work with your team to train the model on your custom entity types, or use a platform that lets you add custom labels.

### Problem 3: Ambiguity

The word "Jordan" could be a country, a person's name, or a basketball player's surname. Without enough context, even a good model gets confused.

**What to do:** Accept that no NER model is perfect. Design your product to handle low-confidence extractions gracefully, perhaps by routing them to a human for review rather than processing them automatically.

### Problem 4: Privacy and sensitive data

NER is very good at extracting personal information — names, addresses, phone numbers. That's powerful, but it creates privacy obligations.

**What to do:** Be intentional about what you extract and store. Know your GDPR, CCPA, or other applicable privacy obligations. Just because NER can extract personal data doesn't mean you should store all of it.

## Build vs. Buy: A PM's Decision Framework

When you're adding NER capability to your product, you have three options:

### Option 1: Use a pre-built model (fastest, usually good enough)

Libraries like **spaCy** (open-source, free) or APIs like **Google Cloud Natural Language** and **AWS Comprehend** offer ready-to-use NER models. You plug them in and they work immediately on common entity types.

Best for: Products where you need standard entity types (people, places, organisations) and your text is reasonably formal.

### Option 2: Fine-tune an existing model on your data (medium effort, better fit)

You take a pre-built model and train it further on examples from your specific domain. This improves accuracy for your use case without building from scratch.

Best for: Products where the standard model underperforms on your specific text type or where you need custom entity categories.

### Option 3: Build a custom model from scratch (most effort, most control)

Your team trains a model entirely on your own labelled data. This gives maximum customisation but requires significant data, time, and ML expertise.

Best for: Highly specialised domains (medical imaging reports, legal contracts in niche jurisdictions) where off-the-shelf models genuinely can't get you there.

As a general rule: **start with option 1, move to option 2 only if the performance gap is causing real user problems.** Most products never need option 3.

## Questions Every PM Should Ask Before Building NER Into Their Product

Before you add NER to your product roadmap, work through these questions with your team:

**1. What entities matter for our users?**
Not all entities are equally valuable. A customer service tool might care about order IDs and product names. A news product cares about people and organisations. Be specific.

**2. What will we do with the extracted entities?**
NER is not an end in itself. What downstream product experience does it enable? Routing? Search? Auto-fill? Alerts? Know the use case before adding the capability.

**3. How accurate does it need to be?**
A support ticket routing system can tolerate occasional misclassifications, but a human will eventually look at the ticket. An automated financial trading signal cannot. Accuracy requirements shape how much investment is justified.

**4. What happens when the model is wrong?**
Design for failure. Build graceful fallbacks, a human review queue, a confidence threshold that routes low-certainty extractions differently.

**5. What are our privacy obligations?**
If you're extracting names, addresses, or other personal data, you need a lawful basis and proper data governance. Involve your legal or compliance team early.

## NER vs. Similar Technologies (Quick Reference)

As a PM, you'll often hear NER mentioned alongside other AI concepts. Here's a cheat sheet:

| Technology | What It Does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **NER** | Identifies named things (people, places, dates) | "Tim Cook" → PERSON |
| **Sentiment Analysis** | Judges if text is positive, negative, or neutral | "I love this product" → POSITIVE |
| **Text Classification** | Puts a whole document into a category | Email → Spam or Not Spam |
| **Keyword Extraction** | Finds important words in text | Article about climate → [climate, temperature, emissions] |
| **Relation Extraction** | Identifies relationships between entities | "Sundar Pichai leads Google" → person LEADS organisation |

These technologies are complementary. A powerful product might combine NER with sentiment analysis — finding that a customer mentioned a specific product name *and* was angry about it.

## A Simple Mental Model to Take With You

Here's a way to explain NER to a colleague who's never heard of it:

> "You know how when you highlight a physical document and use different coloured pens — yellow for names, blue for dates, green for places? NER is software that does that automatically, across millions of documents, in seconds. And then you can search, filter, and build product experiences on top of those highlights."

That's it. That's the core idea.

## What This Means for Your Roadmap

NER is not a flashy AI technology. It won't generate viral demo videos. But it's one of the most practical, quietly valuable AI capabilities you can add to a product.

It turns the raw, messy text your users generate every day to support tickets, reviews, messages, documents and more into structured data you can actually do something with.

Products that unlock value from unstructured text win because most of their competitors are still treating that text as a filing cabinet, something to store, not something to mine.

Understanding NER means you can spot the opportunity, ask the right questions, make the right build-vs-buy call, and design a product experience around what the technology can actually do.

That's a competitive edge that doesn't require you to write a single line of code.

## Conclusion

- A **named entity** is a real-world thing with a name, like a person, place, organisation, date, product, or event
- **NER** reads text and automatically identifies and labels those entities
- It turns unstructured text (emails, reviews, chat) into structured, searchable data
- Common applications include support routing, content monitoring, healthcare records, and financial analysis
- You don't need to understand the math behind NER, you need to understand when to use it, what accuracy to expect, and how to design around its limitations
- Start with pre-built models (spaCy, Google NLP, AWS Comprehend) before considering custom training
- Always ask: what entities matter for my users, what will we do with them, and what happens when the model is wrong?
